Banteng (Tembedau)

Scientific name: Bos javanicus lowi

Conservation Status: Critically Endangered

Estimated Population (Sabah): ~500

Avg. Lifespan: ~25 years

The Banteng, also known as Tembedau is a species of wild cattle native to Southeast Asia, found in parts of Borneo, Java, and mainland regions. In Sabah, it is recognized as the largest land mammal and plays an important role in maintaining forest ecosystems. Bantengs have a sleek reddish-brown coat, white stockings, and curved horns, with males being darker and more muscular than females.

They are herbivores that feed on grasses, bamboo, leaves, fruits, and tree bark, and are commonly found in tropical forests, grasslands, and lowland areas near water. Bantengs live in small herds led by females, becoming more nocturnal in disturbed habitats. Their grazing helps shape vegetation and supports forest regeneration.

Banteng populations have declined sharply due to deforestation, hunting, and genetic isolation. Habitat loss from agriculture and palm oil expansion has fragmented their range, while poaching for meat and horns remains a major threat. In Sabah, fewer than 500 individuals remain. Conservation efforts, including habitat protection, camera trap monitoring, and genetic studies by WWF and local partners, aim to preserve this endangered species.

Quick Facts

Names

Local names: Banteng, Tembedau
Scientific name: Bos javanicus jowi

Diet

Herbivorous: Grass, leaves, bamboo, fruits, tree barks, salt licks

Lifespan

~25 years

Habitat

Grasslands, deciduous & tropical forests, swamp & coastal forests

Behaviour

More active at night in disturbed; grazes openly by day in safe areas

Population

Total: ~3,300
Sabah: ~500

Threats

habitat loss, hunting, hybridization & inbreeding due to isolation

Conservation Efforts

Conservation programmes, genetic preservation, camera traps, GPS tracking
